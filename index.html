How to read your own statement

Every processor formats these differently, and some bury them across three pages. Here's what each line is, in plain English, and what to look for. Work through your own statement with this.

  1. Interchange

    Set by Visa and Mastercard, paid to the bank that issued your customer's card. Nobody can negotiate this, including us. The rate depends on the card type and how the transaction was run.

    Look for: a separate interchange line. If you can't find one, you're on bundled pricing and the markup is hidden inside it.

  2. Assessments and card brand fees

    Small fixed fees paid to the card networks themselves, separate from interchange. Also non-negotiable, and identical no matter who processes your payments.

    Look for: network names — Visa, Mastercard, Discover, Amex — with small percentages attached.

  3. Processor markup

    What your provider adds on top. This is the only part of your rate that is genuinely negotiable, which is exactly why most statements make it hard to isolate.

    Look for: anything left after interchange and assessments. On interchange-plus pricing it's stated outright. On tiered or bundled pricing it isn't stated anywhere.

  4. Non-qualified downgrades

    Transactions that failed to meet the lowest rate you were quoted and got repriced higher. Common causes: a manually keyed card, a missing zip code, a corporate or rewards card, or a batch settled late. The quoted rate can be honest and your real cost still lands far above it.

    Look for: "non-qual," "mid-qual," "EIRF," "standard," or "downgrade." This is usually the single biggest gap between the rate you were sold and the rate you pay.

  5. Per-item and batch fees

    A flat charge on every transaction, plus a charge each time you settle the day's batch. Pennies individually, and they matter enormously if your average ticket is small.

    Look for: a per-transaction amount multiplied by your transaction count. On a $12 average ticket, thirty cents is another 2.5%.

  6. Monthly minimum

    A floor. If your processing fees for the month come in below it, you're billed the difference anyway. It costs you nothing in a busy month and bites in a slow one.

    Look for: a line that only appears in your quieter months.

  7. PCI compliance and non-compliance fees

    A fee for the security compliance program, billed annually or monthly. Separately, a non-compliance fee is charged when the annual questionnaire hasn't been filed. A lot of owners have never been told the questionnaire exists.

    Look for: "PCI," and check whether you're paying both the program fee and a non-compliance penalty.

  8. Equipment lease

    Usually a separate agreement from your processing contract, often with a different company, and frequently non-cancellable for its full term. Switching processors does not end it.

    Look for: a fixed monthly charge for hardware. Check the original lease term before you assume you can walk away.

Add every fee on the statement, divide by your total card volume, and you have your effective rate. That single number is the only fair way to compare one processor to another.

FAQ

The questions operators actually ask.

What is a cash discount program? Do we have to use it?

Cash discounting is a proven, legal program in all 50 states that lets merchants eliminate their credit card processing fees. It's simple, transparent and effective, just how things should be.

With a true cash discount program, your listed prices are the credit card prices. If a customer chooses to pay with cash, our system automatically applies a discount at the register. While it typically offers the greatest savings, you're free to choose traditional processing instead. We'll set you up with whatever you feel is best for your business, and if you ever want to switch programs, we'll happily help you out.

Why don't I switch to cash discount with my current processor?

There's a reason they never mentioned it. Most banks and national processors either don't offer the program or are content collecting your processing fees. They rarely walk you through the reprogramming or equipment changes, sending you to an automated support line instead. With us, you get a seamless experience from start to finish, with real answers and ongoing support.

Will my customers be annoyed?

Some will notice. Very few walk. What determines the reaction is whether it feels like a surprise. Clear signage at the door and on the counter, and staff who can explain it in one sentence, is the whole game. Where it consistently goes badly is when the price appears at the end of a transaction the customer thought was finished.

You're giving customers the choice between a better price or a more convenient payment option. Most card users happily pay the small fee for the flexibility and rewards their cards provide.

Can I use my existing equipment?

Sometimes, some equipment can be reprogrammed, but in most cases you'll need new hardware. We'll order brand-new hardware and set you up with the most efficient configuration for your needs. Many businesses we work with qualify for free equipment or discounted pricing.

I'm locked into a current contract — can I switch?

We understand some contracts are hard to exit. In our experience many can be cancelled for $300–$500, and the monthly processing savings usually cover that cancellation fee easily — leaving more money in your pocket by year-end.

Bring your current agreement along with your most recent processing statement and we'll review the exit terms together. Sometimes the right move is to wait out the contract. If that's the best move for you, we'll let you know — not pressure you to switch.

Do I need to sign a long-term contract?

No long-term contracts. We believe in earning your business every month, the honest way.

How long does switching take?

Usually under a week from application to a working terminal on your counter. Underwriting takes a day or two, equipment is fulfilled within 24 to 48 hours of approval, and it arrives fully programmed and encrypted.

What businesses is this for?

Any business that takes credit cards can benefit. That includes restaurants, cafés, retail, salons, gyms, contractors, auto shops, medical practices, lawyers, accountants, e-commerce and more.
